## Ben Sasse's Evolving Journey: From Senator to University President and Back to Family

Ben Sasse, a prominent figure in American politics and higher education, has recently garnered attention with his resignation from the University of Florida presidency. This latest development highlights a career defined by notable transitions and a deep commitment to both his professional responsibilities and personal values, especially family well-being.

### Quick Context

Ben Sasse announced on January 18, 2024, his decision to step down as the 13th president of the University of Florida, a position he had assumed on February 6, 2023. This unexpected departure, effective January 31, 2024, was prompted by a deeply personal reason: his wife Melissa Sasse's worsening health condition. Melissa has been contending with the long-term effects of strokes sustained in 2007 and recently received a diagnosis of epilepsy alongside new memory issues. Sasse underscored the necessity of prioritizing his family—including their two college-aged daughters and a 13-year-old son—recognizing the intense demands of leading a top national university.

#### Key Transition: From Senator to University Leadership

Sasse's presidency at the University of Florida began after his resignation from the U.S. Senate on January 8, 2023, where he had represented Nebraska since 2015. His transition to the academic realm, a return to his background as a former president of Midland University, was viewed as an opportunity to innovate in higher education. Following his Senate departure, Nebraska Governor Jim Pillen appointed Pete Ricketts to fill the vacancy. Ricketts is now campaigning to retain this seat for the remainder of Sasse's original term, which Sasse had won in 2020.

#### Personal Challenges: Melissa Sasse's Health

The primary reason for his resignation from the University of Florida underscores the often-unseen personal struggles faced by public figures. Sasse's decision to step down from a high-profile position for his wife's health highlights a widely resonant commitment to family. He spoke poignantly about the necessity of spending "more hours of every week" with his "dearest friend," after years where his demanding career led him to miss "too many family dinners, little league games, hugs, and tears."

### What You Need to Know

Ben Sasse's career is marked by a distinctive blend of academic rigor, political engagement, and institutional leadership. Born on February 22, 1972, he is 52 years old as of early 2024. His intellectual foundation was established at Harvard, where he earned a bachelor's degree in government in 1994. He later pursued graduate studies at Yale University, receiving a Master of Arts, Master of Philosophy, and a Ph.D. in history by 2004. His doctoral dissertation was titled "The Anti-Madalyn Majority: Secular Left, Religious Right, and the Rise of Reagan's America."

#### Academic and Early Career Foundations

Prior to his Senate career, Sasse served as Assistant Secretary for Planning and Evaluation in the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services (HHS) during the Bush administration from 2007 to 2009. His leadership capabilities became particularly evident during his tenure as the 15th president of Midland University from 2010 to 2014. He is widely credited with revitalizing the struggling institution, notably increasing enrollment from 590 to 1,300 students and improving its financial and academic standing.

#### A Senator's Political Landscape: Key Stances and Controversies

As a U.S. Senator from Nebraska, Ben Sasse carved out a reputation as a conservative unafraid to challenge his own party. He was a vocal critic of the Affordable Care Act (ACA), famously declaring himself "the anti-Obamacare candidate" during his 2014 Senate campaign. While largely aligning with Republican voting patterns, Sasse frequently expressed dissent, especially regarding former President Donald Trump. He notably voted to convict Trump in his second impeachment trial in January 2021, and publicly criticized Trump for "mocking allies behind closed doors," treating the presidency "like a business opportunity," and "flirting with white supremacists." His criticisms led to a censure from the Lincoln County Republican Party, to which Sasse famously retorted that "politics isn't the weird worship of one dude."

Sasse's political positions covered a wide spectrum: he is pro-life, advocated for congressional term limits, expressed concerns about China's global influence (particularly regarding the Huawei CFO arrest and the origins of COVID-19), and supported increased military assistance for Ukraine without direct U.S. intervention. He also voted against the bipartisan First Step Act criminal justice reform legislation and maintained a strong pro-gun stance, earning an "AQ" rating from the NRA-PVF.

#### The Florida Chapter: Leadership and Scrutiny

Ben Sasse's move to Florida was initially met with some controversy on the University of Florida campus due to his conservative political background. As Ben Sasse began this new chapter in Florida, he lived on campus with his family. His presidency, though brief, was not without scrutiny. An audit by the Florida Auditor General raised concerns about his office's spending, including above-market-rate salaries for former congressional staffers and private jet flights lacking clear business purposes. Despite these issues and student protests, Sasse continued to teach college classes at the university's Hamilton Center—a civic program focused on Western civilization and free society principles—even after his resignation.
